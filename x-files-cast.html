The X-Files, which first aired in 1993, remains an iconic crime thriller worldwide, and there are plenty of justifiable reasons for its ongoing popularity.

The X-Files' cast not only gained global recognition, but also pocketed a significant chunk of change due to its enduring success.

The X-Files is not your average crime procedural, nor one which solely makes viewers feel good via escapism — it also addresses eye-opening social issues to which viewers can relate.

There are two main protagonists on the show – FBI Agents Dana Scully (Gillian Anderson) and Fox Mulder (David Duchovny) – both of whom have vastly different opinions on the way the world works.

Fox Mulder leans toward a belief in the supernatural, while Dana Scully famously favors a pragmatic, scientific approach to phenomena.

Intelligent, thrilling, and nail-biting turns were consistent throughout the entire series' run. Science fiction and paranormal phenomena added to the story, making The X-Files a blockbuster series the world over.

Nine seasons of the show aired between its 1993 debut and 2001. But due to extensive, sustained public demand, the show was rebooted in 2016 – after almost 14 years off the air – and it became the seventh most-watched series in the world.

On April 3rd, several news outlets reported that Gillian Anderson – who previously said she didn't plan to revisit her role – had changed course, indicating that she was "not saying no" to another go-around on the show:

"It’s so funny because for most of my life since I finished 'The X-Files,' every interview I do, people have asked, and the answer has always been, 'Nope, not going to happen.'

"Now, Ryan Coogler, who’s the director of ‘Black Panther,’ brilliant, brilliant director, has approached ('X-Files' creator) Chris Carter to say that he wants to do a take on it. And I cannot think of a better way around for a reboot to happen. I think he’s a bit of a genius."

Two days prior, Variety covered current efforts to reboot The X-Files, and creator Chris Carter (who is not involved) was quoted as surmising that the show's storylines would be trickier to handle in 2024, due to the proliferation of conspiracy theories:

Everything’s a conspiracy ... No one knows what the truth is. It’s completely subjective and relative now.

In light of all this buzz, let's explore The X-Files' cast's net worths, and what they're up to right now, six years after the show ended a second time in 2018.

2 Gillian Anderson

Gillian Anderson's Net Worth (Estimated): $40 Million

Queen of The X-Files Gillian Anderson first started acting on the series when she was just 25 years old; the series last wrapped when she was 50.

In those 25 years (half of her life), Anderson's character, Dana Scully, impacted pop culture more than any of her other roles.

A Golden Globe winner (for The X-Files) in the category of "[Best] Actress In A Drama TV Series," she portrayed Dana Scully, an FBI agent who favored a belief in science over the supernatural.

Her portrayal of Agent Scully measurably changed the lives of many women, as evidenced by remarkably extensive research into the impact of the character on female fans [PDF].

According to that research, Gillian Anderson caused what they dubbed "The Scully Effect," inspiring women to pursue careers in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) fields in droves.

As it turns out, the character's influence on women was unexpectedly significant. Study of "The Scully Effect" was conducted by 21st Century Fox, The Geena Davis Institute On Gender In Media, and J. Walter Thompson Intelligence.

In the course of the research, they identified several shocking statistics, finding that 50 percent of women familiar with Scully's intelligent and courageous character were inspired by her to pursue careers in STEM fields.

Moreover, almost two-thirds of the women polled (63 percent) said "she increased their belief in the importance of STEM" — and "a greater percentage of medium/heavy viewers of The X-Files strongly believe that young women should be encouraged to study STEM than non/light viewers."

In other words, the show's biggest fans tended to develop a belief that more women belonged in STEM than those who watched casually.
